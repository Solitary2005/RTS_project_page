DexGS: Large-Scale Reverse Trajectory Synthesis for Goal-Free Dexterous Grasping

A comparison of the proposed reverse strategy with the conventional forward trajectory. The former utilizes a confirmed high-quality final grasp pose as the starting point, with the complete grasping sequence being generated in reverse order back to the initial approach position. In contrast, the latter cannot determine whether the final grasp will be accurately positioned.


Abstract

The development of general-purpose robots relies heavily on data-driven approaches for mastering dexterous manipulation. However, the performance of these methods is often bottlenecked by the extreme scarcity of high-quality, large-scale trajectory data. Due to the high degrees of freedom (DoF) and the vast diversity of target objects, generating stable grasping sequences remains a significant challenge. Existing approaches are often constrained by the labor-intensive nature of human demonstrations or the exploration inefficiency of reinforcement learning, which struggle to provide sufficient data for robust model training. To address these challenges, we propose a novel reverse trajectory strategy (RTS) which efficiently synthesizes grasp sequences by starting from verified high-quality final grasp poses and generating trajectories in reverse order, which are then reversed to produce natural approaching motions. Leveraging this strategy, we construct DexGS, a large-scale dataset containing 5 million physically plausible grasp sequences across thousands of objects, surpassing existing resources in both scale and dynamic diversity. Building upon this dataset, we introduce the Grasp Sequence Transformer (GST), a sequence prediction model that operates solely on the hand–object spatial relationship and object point clouds, directly outputting complete grasping trajectories without requiring any pre-defined target pose. This goal-free formulation enhances adaptability and generalization in dexterous manipulation. Experimental results in the SAPIEN physics engine and real-world scenarios demonstrate that our approach significantly improves grasping success rates and generalization capabilities. The code and dataset will be released in our project page: https://github.com/Solitary2005/RTS-Grasp.
